Places in Japan



Kanto

The Kanto Region (関東, Kantō, literally "east of the border") contains Japan's largest plain and is very densely populated. The large metropolises of Tokyo and Yokohama are located in the Kanto Region which consists of seven prefectures.

Hokkaido

Hokkaido (北海道, Hokkaidō) is the second largest, northernmost and least developed of Japan's four main islands. Its weather is harsh in winter with lots of snowfall, below zero temperatures and frozen seas, while in summer it does not get as hot and humid as in the other parts of the country.

Kansai

The Kinki Region (近畿地方, Kinki Chihō), also commonly known as Kansai (関西, literally "west of the border") encompasses the Osaka Plain and consists of seven prefectures. It used to be the political and cultural center of Japan for many centuries and includes the cities of Kyoto, Osaka, Nara and Kobe.

Tohuku

The Tohoku Region (東北地方, Tōhoku Chihō, literally "North East Region") consists of six prefectures in the north of Japan's largest island, Honshu. It is well known for its countryside, mountains, lakes, hot springs, high quality rice and rough winters.

Kyushu

Kyushu (九州, Kyūshū, literally "nine provinces") is Japan's third largest island, located southwest of the main island Honshu. An early center of Japanese civilization, Kyushu offers many historic treasures, modern cities and natural beauty.

Kanto

Kanto is a region in eastern Japan that includes the prefectures of Tokyo, Kanagawa, Saitama, Chiba, Ibaraki, Tochigi, and Gunma. Tokyo, the capital of Japan, is located in this region and is one of the world's most populous cities. Kanto has a diverse range of attractions, from the high-tech cityscape of Tokyo to the natural beauty of Nikko National Park. The region is also known for its festivals, such as the Nebuta Festival in Aomori and the Kanto Festival in Akita.

Kansai

Kansai is a region in western Japan that includes the prefectures of Kyoto, Osaka, Hyogo, Nara, Wakayama, and Shiga. This region is known for its rich cultural heritage and historical significance. Kyoto, the former capital of Japan, is home to numerous temples, shrines, and gardens that are recognized as UNESCO World Heritage sites. Osaka is a bustling city with a vibrant food scene, while Nara is famous for its deer park and ancient temples.

Tohuku

Tohoku is a region in northeastern Japan that includes the prefectures of Aomori, Akita, Iwate, Miyagi, Yamagata, and Fukushima. This region is known for its rugged coastline, natural hot springs, and winter sports. Tohoku is also home to numerous festivals and cultural events, such as the Nebuta Festival in Aomori and the Sendai Tanabata Festival in Miyagi. The region has a rich history and is dotted with ancient temples, shrines, and castles.

Kyushu

Kyushu is Japan's southernmost prefecture, known for its warm climate, beautiful beaches, and active volcanoes. The island is home to numerous hot springs and spa resorts, making it a popular destination for relaxation and wellness. Kyushu is also famous for its food, including tonkotsu ramen, mentaiko (spicy cod roe), and shochu (a distilled alcohol). The island has a rich history and is home to many historical sites, such as Kumamoto Castle and Nagasaki's Atomic Bomb Museum.

Hokkaido

Located in the northernmost part of Japan, Hokkaido is a beautiful island that offers a unique blend of natural beauty and modern amenities. With its vast expanses of untouched wilderness, including national parks and wildlife reserves, Hokkaido is a nature lover's paradise. Visitors can enjoy hiking, skiing, snowboarding, and other outdoor activities, as well as exploring the island's many cultural attractions. In addition to its natural beauty, Hokkaido is also known for its delicious cuisine. The island is famous for its seafood, including fresh crab, salmon, and sea urchin. Visitors can also sample local specialties like ramen, miso soup, and sushi, as well as enjoying a variety of international cuisines in the island's cities.
